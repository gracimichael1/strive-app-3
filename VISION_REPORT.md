# STRIVE Vision Report

## Core User Journey

### Step 1: First Open (0-60 seconds)
**Context**: A parent downloads Strive because another gym parent mentioned it, or they Googled "understand my daughter's gymnastics score." They are skeptical. They have tried fitness apps before and abandoned them.

**What happens**: Splash screen loads. No signup wall. They see "See Your Score. Own Your Growth." They tap to set up a profile — daughter's name, level (Xcel Gold), gender, primary events. This takes 20 seconds. The app feels premium immediately: dark background, gold accents, clean typography. It does not feel like a free app.

**Emotion**: Curiosity. "This looks serious."

### Step 2: First Analysis (Minutes 1-5)
**Context**: They have a video on their phone from last Saturday's meet. They have watched it six times already, trying to figure out why she got an 8.75.

**What happens**: They upload the video. The app compresses it, sends it to the analysis engine. Within 30-90 seconds, they see a score — and it is close to what the judge gave. But more importantly, they see WHY. "Leg separation 15 degrees on back handspring (-0.10)." "Insufficient amplitude on back layout (-0.15)." Each deduction has a name, a timestamp, a severity color, and they can tap to jump to that exact moment in the video.

**Emotion**: Revelation. "Oh my God, I can actually SEE it now." This is the dopamine hit. They did not know what a leg separation deduction looked like. Now they do. They watch the timestamped moment three times. They see it. They feel like an insider.

### Step 3: Second Analysis (Days 2-7)
**Context**: Practice is Tuesday. Parent records a routine on their phone through the viewing window. They upload it that night.

**What happens**: They see the same deductions from the meet — but maybe the leg separation is smaller. Or maybe there is a new one they had not noticed. They start to see patterns. "She always bends her knees on the back layout." The app is teaching them to see what coaches see.

**Emotion**: Engagement. Investment. "I am learning something real about my kid's sport."

### Step 4: Habit Formation (Weeks 2-6)
**Context**: They are now recording routines at practice regularly. They have a small library of analyses. They are starting to see trends.

**What happens**: Before a meet, they review the last three analyses. They know the two deductions their daughter needs to fix. After the meet, they upload the competition routine and compare. The score tracks. They are not guessing anymore.

**Emotion**: Competence. Confidence. They feel like they understand the sport at a level they never did before. At meets, they notice deductions in OTHER gymnasts' routines.

### Step 5: Evangelism (Weeks 4-8)
**Context**: They are sitting next to another parent at a meet. That parent says "I don't understand how she got an 8.6, that looked great to me."

**What happens**: The Strive parent pulls out their phone. "Here, look at this." They show the analysis of their own daughter's routine. The other parent sees the deduction breakdown, the video timestamps, the severity colors. Their jaw drops.

**Emotion**: Status. Generosity. "I know something you don't, and I'm going to share it." This is the viral moment. It happens in person, at the gym, between parents who already trust each other.

## The One Metric That Predicts Retention

**Analyses per month.**

Not sessions. Not screen time. Not logins. Analyses.

Each analysis requires the parent to (a) have a video, (b) care enough to upload it, and (c) engage with the results. It is a high-intent action. A parent who analyzes 0 videos in month 2 is gone. A parent who analyzes 1 is at risk. A parent who analyzes 3+ is locked in.

**Why 3 is the magic number:**

A competitive gymnast typically has 1-2 meets per month and 3-4 practices per week. A parent doing 3 analyses per month is recording at least one practice routine AND their meet routines. That means:
- They are forming a recording habit at practice
- They are comparing practice to competition
- They are tracking improvement over time
- They have enough data to see patterns

At 3 analyses/month, the app transitions from "tool I tried" to "part of how I support my daughter's gymnastics." The data becomes personal. Deleting the app means losing their daughter's progress history. That is the retention lock.

**Implication for the Free tier**: 3 free analyses per month is not a teaser — it is exactly the habit threshold. Parents who use all 3 will convert to Pro because they want to analyze practice routines on top of meet routines. Parents who do not use all 3 were never going to convert anyway.

## Five Features That Would Generate Word-of-Mouth in Gyms

### 1. Meet Day Score Predictor
Before the meet, the parent uploads their daughter's best recent practice routine. Strive gives a predicted score range: "Based on this routine, expect 8.85-9.10 on floor today." After the meet, they enter the actual score. When the prediction is close, the parent WILL show the other parent sitting next to them. "It predicted 8.95 and she got 9.0." This is a shareable, verifiable, impressive result.

### 2. Side-by-Side Comparison ("Then vs. Now")
Upload a routine from September and one from March. Strive shows them on a split screen with matching timestamps. The deduction list shows what was fixed and what remains. Parents already make these comparisons in their heads — making it visual and concrete turns invisible progress into something they can screenshot and text to grandma.

### 3. Pre-Meet Focus Card
A single-screen printable card: "Ava's Top 3 Things to Remember on Beam Today." Generated from the most frequent deductions in recent analyses. Simple enough for a 10-year-old to read in the car on the way to the meet. Coaches give verbal reminders. This makes it tangible. Parents will photograph this card and post it. Coaches will notice and ask about it.

### 4. Event Milestone Badges
"Ava's floor score has improved 0.45 since October." A shareable badge with her name, event, and improvement delta. Not gamification for its own sake — real, measured progress tied to real scores. Parents share progress. It is what they do. Give them something worth sharing that is not a generic "7-day streak" badge.

### 5. "What the Judge Saw" Replay Mode
Slow-motion replay of the routine with deduction callouts appearing in real time, like a sports broadcast. Gold border around the video. Severity colors pulsing at the moment of each deduction. This is the feature parents show each other at practice. It turns a phone video into a broadcast-quality analysis. The visual quality alone — Olympic broadcast aesthetic, not amateur app — makes people ask "What IS that?"

## The "Wow Moment"

The parent uploads a video of their daughter's floor routine from last weekend's meet. She scored an 8.85 and the parent thought it looked great.

Forty-five seconds later, Strive returns a score of 8.80 and a list of 9 deductions.

The parent taps the first one: "Leg separation on round-off back handspring, -0.10." The video jumps to that exact moment. The parent watches it in slow motion. They see the legs come apart. They have watched this routine fifteen times and never noticed.

They tap the next one: "Insufficient height on back layout, -0.15." They see it. The layout was low. They had no idea what "good height" looked like until this moment.

**The feeling is**: "I have been watching this sport for three years and I could never see what the judges see. Now I can."

It happens in under 90 seconds. The score is close to the real score (that is the credibility). The deductions are specific and visible (that is the education). The video jumps to the exact moment (that is the proof).

The parent does not need to be told the app is valuable. They just experienced it. They will text their gym parent group chat within the hour.

## Three Ways to Deepen the Coach Relationship

### 1. Position Strive as the Coach's Homework Enforcer, Not a Second Opinion
Coaches already tell gymnasts what to fix. The problem is that the gymnast forgets by Tuesday, and the parent never heard it in the first place. Strive reinforces what the coach is already saying. When a parent comes to the coach and says "Strive flagged leg separation on the back handspring, is that something you're working on?" — the coach feels supported, not undermined. The messaging must be: "Strive helps your gymnast remember what Coach already told them."

**Concrete feature**: A "Coach's Focus" field where the parent can type in what the coach said to work on ("keep legs together on tumbling, stick landings"). Strive highlights those specific deductions in results with a special "Coach's Priority" tag. Now the app is amplifying the coach's voice, not competing with it.

### 2. Give Coaches a Reason to Recommend It: Parent Education Reduces Coaching Friction
The single biggest frustration for a gymnastics coach is parents who do not understand scoring and complain about "unfair" judges. A parent who has used Strive for a month understands that an 8.85 with specific deductions is a real score, not a conspiracy. Coaches should hear from Strive (via a short email or one-pager they can share at parent meetings): "When your gym parents understand deductions, they stop questioning scores and start supporting training." Position it as: "This makes your parent meetings easier."

### 3. Team Accounts Where Coaches See Aggregate Trends (Not Individual Scores)
Offer a free coach dashboard that shows anonymized, aggregate data: "Your Xcel Gold team's most common deductions this month: leg separation (34%), landing steps (28%), insufficient amplitude (22%)." The coach did not have to film anything or change their workflow. The data came from parents who were already using the app. The coach gets actionable intel for practice planning. The coach becomes an advocate because the app is making them better at their job — and it cost them nothing.

## The Habit Loop

### Cue
The parent sits down at practice or arrives at a meet and takes out their phone to record their daughter's routine. The act of recording is already a habit for most gym parents. The cue is: **"I just recorded a routine."**

The phone is already in their hand. The video is already in their camera roll. The app is one tap away.

### Routine
Open Strive. Upload the video. Wait 45-90 seconds. Review the deductions. Tap through 2-3 of them to watch the video moments. Total time: 3-5 minutes.

This fits into dead time that already exists: sitting in the car after practice, waiting for the next rotation at a meet, lying in bed after the kids are asleep.

### Reward
**Variable reward, three layers:**

1. **The score** — Did it go up from last time? Even 0.05 improvement feels meaningful. This is the slot-machine pull.
2. **The insight** — "Oh, THAT'S what the leg separation looks like." Each analysis teaches the parent something new about the sport. Knowledge is a reward.
3. **The conversation** — The parent now has something specific to say. To their daughter: "Your layouts are getting so much higher." To the coach: "We've been working on the leg separation." To other parents: "Have you tried this app?" The social reward sustains the habit.

### Making It Automatic
- Push notification after meet weekends: "How did [Gymnast Name] do? Upload her routines while they're fresh."
- After 3 analyses, show a "Progress" tab that only gets more valuable with more data. Stopping means losing the trendline.
- The 3-free-per-month limit creates urgency: "I should use my free analyses this month before they reset."

## Emotional Design Principles

### For the Parent

**After uploading**: Anticipation, not anxiety. The loading state should feel like waiting for a score at a meet — tension that resolves into clarity. Use the gold shimmer animation. Never show a generic spinner.

**After seeing results**: Empowerment. Not judgment. The parent should feel like they just gained X-ray vision, not like they just received a report card. The language is: "Here's what happened and here's what to work on" — never "Here's what went wrong."

**After comparing over time**: Pride. The progress view should make a parent feel the same thing they feel watching their daughter nail a skill she has been struggling with. Show improvement prominently. Use language like "Since October, Ava has eliminated 3 recurring deductions."

**The overall emotional arc**: Confused outsider who becomes an informed insider. The parent goes from "I don't understand this sport" to "I can see what the judges see." That transformation is the product.

### For the 12-Year-Old Gymnast

**She should feel like an athlete being scouted, not a student being graded.** The analysis should feel like what she sees on TV during the Olympics — broadcast graphics, gold colors, her name in a clean font next to her scores. Not a red-ink report card.

**Specific deductions should feel fixable, not permanent.** "Leg separation on back handspring (-0.10)" should feel like a specific thing she can fix at practice tomorrow, not a character flaw. The severity colors help here: green and yellow deductions feel manageable. The language should always imply agency: "Work on keeping legs together through the back handspring" — she can DO something about it.

**Improvement should feel celebrated.** When a deduction disappears from one analysis to the next, that deserves a moment. A subtle animation, a "Fixed" badge, a progress indicator. She should feel like the app noticed her hard work.

**She should want to show her friends.** The "What the Judge Saw" replay mode, the event badges, the progress cards — these should be visually impressive enough that a 12-year-old would show her teammate at practice. "Look at my analysis." If the design is good enough, the gymnasts become a distribution channel alongside the parents.

### Emotions to Avoid

- **Shame**: A bad score should never feel like a personal failure. The framing is always: "Here are specific things to improve." Never: "Here are your mistakes."
- **Overwhelm**: Nine deductions can feel crushing if presented as a wall of red. Progressive disclosure matters. Show the score first, then the top 3 priorities, then the full list for those who want it.
- **Distrust**: If the AI score is wildly off from the real score, the parent loses faith instantly and never comes back. Accuracy within 0.15 of meet scores is not a nice-to-have. It is the entire foundation of trust. One bad analysis can undo five good ones.
- **Boredom**: The app should never feel clinical or sterile. Every screen should have the energy of a broadcast — gold accents, clean motion, the feeling that something important is happening. This is her sport. Treat it with the production value it deserves.
